Carl Sagan

The man who aroused the curiosity in science to millions

Here's a time line of Carl Sagan's life

  • From 1955 to 1962 -Sagan attended the University of Chicago, where he earned a bachelor’s and a master’s degree in physics in 1955 and 1956, respectively, and a doctorate in astronomy and astrophysics in 1962.
  • From 1962 to 1968 -He worked at Harvard University and the Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory.
  • 1968 -He became the director of Cornell University’s Laboratory for Planetary Studies
  • 1971 -Sagan became a full professor in Cornell University.
  • 1973 -He published, with Jerome Agel, The Cosmic Connection: An Extraterrestrial Perspective, which earned him prominence as a popular science writer.
  • 1980 -Sagan cofounded the Planetary Society, an international nonprofit organization for space exploration. That same year he reached the height of his public fame with the television series Cosmos, which he wrote with his wife, Ann Druyan.
  • 1983 -He cowrote the paper that introduced the concept of “nuclear winter,” a catastrophic global cooling that would result from a nuclear war. Sagan was also coauthor of The Cold and the Dark: The World After Nuclear War (1984).
  • 1994 -Sagan was awarded the Public Welfare Medal by the National Academy of Sciences, but he never succeeded in becoming a member of that prestigious academy.
  • 1996 -He argued strongly against tendencies toward pseudoscience and occultism, most comprehensively in his last major book, The Demon-Haunted World. This year he dies at the age of 62.
